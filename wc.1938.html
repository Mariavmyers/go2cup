The World Cup Through History · Chapter 4

1942 & 1946: The Lost World Cups

Two World Cups were scheduled. Neither was played. The world the tournament lived in had ceased to exist — and football paid its own quiet, terrible price.

● No tournament held — 1942 & 1946

The silence

There was no World Cup in 1942, and none in 1946. World War II forced FIFA to abandon both — and because the 1938 final had been the last one played, Italy stayed the reigning world champions for a record sixteen years, from their first title in 1934 all the way until 1950.

Germany and Brazil had both bid to host in 1942. With two straight tournaments already held in Europe, many believed Brazil was likely to win the vote, and planning had begun. Then, in September 1939, Hitler invaded Poland. There was no cancellation announcement and no committee vote — the World Cup simply stopped existing, because the world around it had.

Football at war

The game didn't vanish. It scattered — into uniforms, occupied cities, prison camps and resistance cells. These are a few of the people who carried it, and what the war did to them.

The trophy that survived in a shoebox

With Rome under occupation, Ottorino Barassi — the Italian vice-president of FIFA — quietly removed the Jules Rimet Trophy from a bank and hid it in a shoebox under his bed. The golden symbol of world football sat out the entire war there, out of reach of occupying troops.

Rino Della Negra — Red Star winger, executed 1944

Rather than be sent to forced labour in Germany, this young French-Italian winger for Red Star joined the armed resistance in Paris. Captured and wounded, he was executed by firing squad at Mont-Valérien in February 1944 as part of the famous "Affiche Rouge" group. His final letter ended with a message to his teammates. A stand at Red Star's stadium now carries his name.

Alexandre Villaplane — and the road not taken

Villaplane had captained France in the very first World Cup match in 1930. During the occupation he chose the opposite path to Della Negra: he joined the French Gestapo, hunted resistance fighters, and was made an SS officer. He was executed for his crimes by firing squad in December 1944 — the same year, and the same fate, as the footballer who resisted.

Kyiv, 1942 — the "Death Match," as it really happened

On 9 August 1942, in occupied Kyiv, FC Start — built largely from former Dynamo Kyiv players working at a bread factory — beat a German military side, Flakelf, 5–3. The popular legend says they were shot on the spot for winning. The records say otherwise: arrests came more than a week later, after Start thrashed another team 8–0 and a rival coach denounced them, with the Gestapo suspecting the Dynamo men of NKVD ties. Several were sent to the Syrets camp, where about four eventually died. Their fate was tied to who they were suspected of being — not the scoreline. The myth grew because the courage underneath it was real.

Britain's footballers go to war

At Bolton's Burnden Park in April 1939, captain Harry Goslin stood before the crowd and announced the whole team would enlist. They did, joining the 53rd (Bolton) Field Regiment. Goslin rose to lieutenant and was killed by shrapnel on the Italian front in December 1943. Arsenal lost nine players — the heaviest toll of any English club — among them Bobby Daniel, an RAF flight sergeant gunner lost on a bombing raid over Berlin on Christmas Eve 1943.

1946 — Too soon for a world in ruins

When Germany surrendered in May 1945, much of Europe lay in rubble. Cities were flattened, millions were displaced, and governments were consumed with feeding the living and burying the dead — not staging football tournaments.

And even if FIFA had wanted to hold a 1946 World Cup, the question was: who would play? Germany and Japan were banned and would be barred from 1950 too. Whole national squads had lost players to the war. The infrastructure itself — the stadiums, the leagues — had been bombed away across half the continent.

Árpád Weisz — a great manager, murdered

Weisz, a Hungarian Jew, had been one of Europe's pioneering coaches, winning league titles in Italy. He, his wife and his two young children were deported to Auschwitz, where they were murdered. His name was largely forgotten for decades until researchers pieced his story back together.

Leo Goldstein — survived Auschwitz because he knew the rules

A Polish Jew marched toward the gas chambers, Goldstein survived after he was pulled aside to referee matches for the camp's guards. He lived, emigrated, and became a FIFA international referee — going on to work the 1962 World Cup in Chile, including the infamous "Battle of Santiago." One of the most extraordinary second acts in the history of the game.

The women who kept the game alive

The English FA had banned women from playing on its members' grounds back in 1921 — a ban that would last fifty years. Yet through the war, women's factory and works teams kept football going, drawing real crowds and raising money for war relief, often on whatever pitches would have them. Their contribution was large, and for a long time almost entirely written out of the official history.

Brazil steps forward

At the 1946 FIFA Congress, Brazil made an offer that may have saved the tournament: give us until 1950, and we will host — and build a stadium the world has never seen. That promise became the Maracanã. Without it, the World Cup might have stayed suspended indefinitely.

The cost, and the return

Before the game could even come back, it was struck again: in May 1949, the Superga air disaster killed the entire Grande Torino team — the spine of Italy's national side — just months before the 1950 finals.

The cancellation of 1942 and 1946 wasn't only a logistical decision. It was an admission that football, like the world around it, needed time to grieve and rebuild — nation by nation, club by club, player by player. When the World Cup finally returned in Brazil in 1950, it was more than a tournament. It was proof the world had found a way back.

The 1942 and 1946 World Cups were never really cancelled. They were consumed — stadiums became barracks, players became soldiers, and the game went silent while the guns spoke.
