Best Neighborhoods to Stay in Mexico City for the World Cup

March 19, 2026 · 6 min read · Maria Myers · GO2CUP

Mexico City is one of the most electrifying host cities for the 2026 FIFA World Cup — a megalopolis of 22 million people, world-class cuisine, centuries of history, and some of the best street energy on the planet. But where you stay will define your entire experience. Get it right and you'll walk out your door into the beating heart of the city. Get it wrong and you'll spend your match days burning time in traffic or feeling unsafe after dark.

We've broken down the five neighborhoods that make the most sense for World Cup visitors, based on safety, price, transit access to Estadio Azteca, and the kind of atmosphere each delivers. Whether you're traveling on a shoestring or flying business class, there's a right answer here for you.

Quick tip: Estadio Azteca is in the south of the city. All transit times below are via Mexico City Metro — the cheapest, most reliable option for match days. Book accommodation early; World Cup dates will sell out months in advance.

The 5 Best Neighborhoods in Detail

1. Roma Norte

The social hub — where the World Cup energy will be loudest

Roma Norte is the neighborhood that gets talked about most among international visitors — and for good reason. It's walkable, photogenic, and absolutely alive with restaurants, rooftop bars, independent coffee shops, and mezcalerias that stay buzzing until the early hours. Streets are lined with early-20th-century mansions converted into boutique hotels and apartments, giving it a character unlike anything you'll find in newer districts.

For World Cup 2026, expect Roma Norte to be the social nerve center for traveling fans. Fan parties, informal watch gatherings, and street celebrations will flood this area on match nights. The neighborhood's density of bars means you'll never be far from a screen or a crowd.

2. Condesa

The peaceful twin — all the charm, less of the chaos

Condesa sits directly adjacent to Roma Norte and shares much of the same DNA — great food, independent cafes, a young professional crowd — but with a notably calmer atmosphere. Tree-lined boulevards, Art Deco architecture, and Parque México give it a residential elegance that's genuinely relaxing after the intensity of match days. It's consistently ranked among the safest neighborhoods in the capital.

If you're traveling with a partner, young family, or simply want to decompress in quieter surroundings without sacrificing access to the city's best experiences, Condesa is the smarter pick over Roma. You're still close to Paseo de la Reforma and the city's major attractions, and the metro connection south to Azteca follows the same route.

3. Polanco

The luxury district — five-star service, elite security, zero compromises

Polanco is Mexico City's answer to Beverly Hills. This is where the flagship stores of every major international brand line the streets, where the city's finest restaurants cluster (including multiple spots recognized among Latin America's best), and where every major international hotel chain maintains a flagship property. Security here is visible and robust — many streets have private guards, and the neighborhood has an entirely different feel from the rest of the city.

Adjacent to the vast Bosque de Chapultepec park — a genuine urban oasis — Polanco is ideal for travelers who want a completely premium experience and aren't concerned about budget. The Anthropology Museum, one of the world's great cultural institutions, is a short walk away. The transit connection to Estadio Azteca takes slightly longer, but ride-shares from here are plentiful and reliable.

4. Reforma Corridor

The business spine — maximum connectivity, international hotel brands

Paseo de la Reforma is Mexico City's grand boulevard — a wide, tree-lined avenue that cuts diagonally across the city, lined with skyscrapers, monuments, and large-format hotels. Staying here means you're at the geographic and logistical center of the city, with easy metro and Metrobús connections in every direction. The Angel of Independence monument — almost certainly a fan gathering point during the tournament — is right here.

Reforma is pragmatic more than romantic. You won't find as much neighborhood character or local charm here as you will in Roma or Condesa, but you will find supremely functional accommodation, efficient transportation, and easy access to both the historic center and the wealthier western districts. For travelers on longer itineraries who need a reliable base, it's an excellent choice.

5. Centro Histórico

The budget option — history, culture, and the fan zone on your doorstep

The historic center of Mexico City — built on the ruins of Tenochtitlán, the Aztec capital — is one of the most extraordinary urban environments in the Americas. The Zócalo, one of the world's largest public squares, will almost certainly host a major FIFA fan zone during the tournament. Staying here puts you at the epicenter of daytime culture: the Palacio de Bellas Artes, Templo Mayor, the Murales of Diego Rivera, and hundreds of years of layered history all within walking distance.

However, caution is warranted. The historic center has undergone significant improvement in recent years but remains uneven — certain blocks are well-policed and busy, others become deserted and higher-risk after dark. Solo female travelers and those unfamiliar with the city should be especially vigilant at night. That said, for experienced budget travelers who keep their wits about them, Centro offers tremendous value and an authenticity the wealthier neighborhoods simply cannot match.

Which Neighborhood is Right for You?

The honest answer is that Roma Norte or Condesa will serve the majority of World Cup visitors best. They hit the sweet spot of price, safety, atmosphere, and transit access. If you're traveling with a tight group and want the energy of the tournament to surround you 24/7, Roma Norte is your pick. If you want that same level of access but with a calmer base to return to, Condesa wins.

Polanco is the clear choice if budget is not a concern — it's objectively the safest and most comfortable part of the city, and the restaurant scene alone justifies the premium. Reforma makes strong sense for travelers who prioritize logistical efficiency over local flavor, or those booking through corporate travel programs. And Centro Histórico rewards adventurous budget travelers who do their homework on which specific blocks and hotels to choose.

Getting to Estadio Azteca from Any Neighborhood

Estadio Azteca is located in the south of the city in the Xochimilco district. The Metro is by far the best option on match days — it's cheap (under $0.30 USD per journey), extensive, and avoids the surface traffic gridlock that will affect all road vehicles during the tournament.

  • From Roma Norte / Condesa: Take Metro Line 2 westbound toward Tasqueña, then transfer to the Tren Ligero (light rail) southbound to Estadio Azteca. Total journey: 45–60 minutes.
  • From Polanco / Reforma: Take Metro Line 7 or Metrobús to a Line 2 connection, then same route south. Total: 50–60 minutes depending on starting point.
  • From Centro Histórico: Metro Line 2 is directly accessible from several Zócalo stations — head westbound to Tasqueña, then Tren Ligero south. Total: 55–70 minutes.

Plan to arrive at least 90 minutes before kickoff on major match days. Security queues at Azteca can be significant, and the surrounding streets fill up quickly with vendors, fans, and organized activities.
